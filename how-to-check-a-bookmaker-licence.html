How to Check if a Bookmaker is Licensed

A step-by-step guide to verifying that your bookmaker holds a valid South African provincial gambling licence.

Written by Thabo Mokoena Editor Last reviewed: March 2026 Fact-checked by Lerato Dlamini
Why verify? Only bookmakers licensed by a South African provincial gambling board are legal. Unlicensed operators have no obligation to pay your winnings, protect your data, or provide dispute resolution. It takes 5 minutes to verify a licence, and could save you from losing your money.

Step-by-Step Verification

1

Check the Operator's Website

Licensed operators are legally required to display their licence details on their website. Look for: the provincial gambling board name, licence number, and a link to the board's website. This is usually in the footer or on the "About" page.

2

Contact the Provincial Board

If you cannot find licence details, or want to verify them independently, contact the relevant provincial gambling board directly. Provide the operator's name and ask them to confirm the licence status. Contact details for all 9 boards are listed below.

3

Red Flags to Watch For

  • No licence information anywhere on the site
  • Claims to be "licensed in Curacao", "Malta", or another foreign jurisdiction only
  • No South African contact details
  • No FICA/KYC verification required to deposit or withdraw
  • Crypto-only deposits with no Rand option
  • Domain ending in .com without a .co.za alternative

Why Licence Verification Matters

South Africa's gambling market is regulated at the provincial level. Each of the nine provinces has its own gambling board, empowered under the National Gambling Act 7 of 2004 to licence, monitor, and take action against betting operators within its borders. A licensed bookmaker must meet ongoing requirements around player fund protection, responsible gambling tools, and FICA compliance. If something goes wrong, you have a formal channel for complaints.

An unlicensed operator has none of these obligations. There's no legal requirement for them to pay your winnings, keep your funds separate from operating capital, or respond to a dispute. Many unlicensed sites are based offshore and deliberately target South African bettors with rand-denominated accounts while avoiding local regulation. Some simply disappear with balances unpaid.

The five minutes it takes to verify a licence is worth it. You're checking that the entity holding your money is answerable to a South African authority, that player funds are protected, and that you have a real complaints process available to you if a payout is refused or an account is closed unfairly. Every bookmaker reviewed on this site holds a current South African provincial licence, but that doesn't mean every site you encounter online does.

Step-by-Step Licence Check

You can verify any South African bookmaker's licence in under five minutes using publicly available information. Here's how.

1

Find the licence number on the bookmaker's website

Licensed operators are required to display their licence details. Scroll to the footer of the site first, as this is where most bookmakers show the licence number, the issuing provincial board, and sometimes a logo or badge. If it's not in the footer, check the "About Us", "Legal", or "Terms and Conditions" pages. Note the exact licence number and the name of the board listed.

2

Visit the relevant provincial gambling board's website

Use the directory below to find the correct board. The bookmaker's footer usually names the province. If they state "licensed by the Gauteng Gambling Board", go to ggb.org.za. The full list of board websites is in the Legal Hub.

3

Search the public register

Most provincial boards maintain a public register of licensed operators on their website. Look for a section called "Licensees", "Public Register", or "Licensed Operators". Enter the licence number or the bookmaker's trading name to confirm the record exists.

4

Verify the licence holder matches the trading name

The registered entity on the gambling board's records may differ from the brand name you see on the website. For example, a bookmaker trading as "BetExample" might be registered as "Example Betting Pty Ltd". Cross-check the company registration number if you're unsure. A mismatch between the legal entity and the trading name isn't necessarily a problem, but it's worth confirming with the board directly.

5

Check that the licence is current

Licences are issued for a set term and must be renewed. The public register usually shows an expiry date or a status field (active, suspended, lapsed). A licence that was valid two years ago may no longer be. If the status shows "suspended" or "revoked", do not deposit. If you can't find an expiry date, call or email the board directly and ask for a status confirmation. Contact details for all nine boards are in the Legal Hub.

Red Flags of Unlicensed Operators

Not every warning sign is obvious. These are the most common indicators that a betting site may not hold a valid South African licence.

  • No licence number visible anywhere on the site. A licensed operator is legally required to display its licence details. If you can't find a licence number after checking the footer, about page, and terms, that's a significant concern.
  • Foreign jurisdiction only. Sites that claim to be "licensed in Curacao" or "regulated by the Malta Gaming Authority" aren't subject to South African law. Foreign licences don't give you any rights under South African consumer or gambling legislation.
  • No South African phone number or postal address. Licensed bookmakers must maintain a registered address in South Africa. A site with only an overseas address or no physical address at all has no accountable local presence.
  • Cryptocurrency-only deposits. If a site accepts only Bitcoin or other crypto and offers no rand payment option, it's almost certainly not provincially licensed. All licensed South African bookmakers accept rand through local payment methods.
  • No FICA verification required. South African law requires licensed operators to verify the identity of every customer before allowing withdrawals. If a site lets you deposit and withdraw large amounts without asking for any identity documents, it's not complying with local FICA requirements.
  • Bonuses that seem too large to be real. Unlicensed sites sometimes advertise very large welcome bonuses to attract depositors. The bonus terms, if they exist, usually make it impossible to actually withdraw anything. Licensed operators can still offer generous bonuses, but the terms are required to be clear and fair.

If you encounter any of these signs, don't deposit. Check the Legal Hub for a list of verified licensed operators.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the National Gambling Board?

The National Gambling Board (NGB) is a national body established under the National Gambling Act 7 of 2004. It coordinates policy between the nine provincial gambling boards, maintains a national register of licences, and can investigate matters that cross provincial boundaries. The NGB doesn't directly licence bookmakers. That role belongs to each province's gambling board. But it sets the national framework that all provincial boards and operators must follow.

Can I bet on international sites from South Africa?

Technically, placing a bet with an unlicensed offshore operator isn't legal under South African law. The National Gambling Act prohibits interactive gambling on platforms that aren't licensed in South Africa. In practice, enforcement is directed at operators rather than individual bettors, but using an unlicensed site means you have no legal protection if the operator refuses to pay or closes your account. We recommend sticking to provincially licensed bookmakers.

What happens if a licensed bookmaker closes?

If a licensed operator ceases trading, the provincial gambling board that issued the licence oversees the wind-down process. Licensed bookmakers are required to keep player funds separate from operating funds, which means your balance should be protected even if the business becomes insolvent. In practice, recovering funds from a closed operator can take time. The board administers any claims process. This is another reason to stick to licensed operators: unlicensed sites have no such obligations and player funds are typically unrecoverable if the site closes.

How do I file a complaint against a bookmaker?

Start by submitting a formal complaint in writing to the bookmaker itself. Keep a record of all correspondence. If the bookmaker doesn't resolve the issue within a reasonable time, typically 7 to 14 days, escalate to the provincial gambling board that issued their licence. The board has the power to investigate complaints, impose fines, and in serious cases revoke the licence. Contact details for all nine boards are in the table above and on the Legal Hub. For disputes involving amounts over R10,000, you may also consider approaching the National Consumer Commission.
