How odds formats work

Decimal odds

Decimal odds are the standard at South African bookmakers and across most of Europe. The number represents your total return for every R1 staked, including your original stake. Odds of 2.50 mean a R100 bet returns R250 (R150 profit plus your R100 back). The higher the number, the less likely the outcome according to the bookmaker. Anything below 2.00 is odds-on, meaning the bookmaker considers it more likely than not to happen.

Fractional odds

Fractional odds are traditional in the UK and still appear on some horse racing markets in South Africa. They show profit relative to stake. Odds of 3/2 mean you win R3 for every R2 you risk. To convert fractional to decimal, divide the first number by the second and add 1. So 3/2 becomes (3 / 2) + 1 = 2.50 in decimal. Fractions like 1/4 or 2/7 are short-priced favourites, while 10/1 or 20/1 are longshots.

American odds

American odds use plus and minus signs. A positive number like +150 shows how much profit you'd make on a R100 stake. A negative number like -200 shows how much you need to stake to win R100 in profit. Most South African punters won't encounter American odds unless they're reading US-based sports coverage or using an international exchange. The converter above handles both positive and negative values.

FAQ

Which odds format do SA bookmakers use?

Almost all licensed South African sportsbooks display decimal odds by default. Some, like Hollywoodbets, also offer fractional odds on horse racing. You can usually switch formats in your account settings.

What is implied probability?

Implied probability is the percentage chance an outcome has based on the odds. Decimal odds of 2.00 imply a 50% chance (1 / 2.00 = 0.50). The bookmaker's margin means implied probabilities across all outcomes in a market will add up to more than 100%.

Do odds affect my winnings differently in each format?

No. The format is just a different way of displaying the same thing. Decimal 2.50, fractional 3/2, and American +150 all pay exactly the same return on the same stake. Use whichever format you find easiest to read.

Why do odds change before an event?

Bookmakers adjust odds based on betting volume, team news, injuries, and market conditions. If lots of money comes in on one outcome, the bookmaker shortens those odds and pushes the other outcomes out. This is normal and happens continuously up to kick-off.
