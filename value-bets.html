Explainer

What is a Value Bet?

A value bet exists whenever the true probability of an outcome is higher than the probability implied by a bookmaker's odds. Put simply: the bookmaker is offering you more than you should get for how likely the event actually is.

Every set of decimal odds carries an implied probability. To convert: divide 1 by the decimal odds. Odds of 2.00 imply a 50% chance (1 / 2.00 = 0.50). Odds of 2.50 imply 40%. Odds of 1.67 imply 60%.

Now imagine a football match where you estimate Team A has a 60% true chance of winning. If the bookmaker is quoting 2.00 for Team A to win, their implied probability is only 50%. Your estimate is 10 percentage points higher than theirs. That gap is the edge, and that edge is what makes the bet a value bet.

The concept comes from expected value theory. If the true win probability is higher than the implied win probability in the odds, then the expected value of the bet is positive. You will not win every time, but if you consistently find and back these edges, you should turn a profit over a large enough sample of bets.

The opposite is also true. A bet where the bookmaker's implied probability is higher than the true probability has negative expected value. Most casual bets fall into this category, which is how bookmakers stay profitable. Value betting turns that logic on its head by focusing only on situations where the odds are in your favour.

Sharp books attract professional bettors who quickly arbitrage away mispricings, which means their lines reflect genuine probabilities. Soft books cater to recreational bettors and are slower to adjust. When a soft book's odds are higher than the sharp consensus for the same outcome, there is a potential value window.

The Maths

Understanding Expected Value

EV Formula
EV = (Probability × Decimal Odds) − 1
If EV > 0, the bet has positive expected value. If EV < 0, the bet loses money in the long run.

Let's walk through a real example. Team A is playing at home and you estimate their true win probability at 50%. Betway is quoting 2.40 for Team A to win. Plugging into the formula:

EV = (0.50 × 2.40) − 1 = 1.20 − 1 = 0.20

That 0.20 means a 20% edge. For every R100 you stake on this bet in the long run, you should return R120. You won't win this particular bet half the time, but across hundreds of similar bets at this edge level, the profit accumulates.

Now consider the same match at odds of 1.80. The implied probability is 55.6%, but your estimate is only 50%. EV = (0.50 × 1.80) − 1 = −0.10. That's a 10% negative edge. The same team, the same match, but different odds produce the opposite outcome. The odds are everything.

This is why long-term profitability in sports betting comes from finding positive EV consistently, not from picking winners. A bettor who wins 45% of their bets at average odds of 2.30 earns more than one who wins 55% at average odds of 1.50. The edge in the odds matters more than the raw win rate.

It also explains why losing runs don't necessarily mean your strategy is wrong. Variance is normal, especially over small samples. A genuine 10% edge means you'll still lose roughly 50% of individual bets. Track your results over a minimum of 200 to 500 bets before drawing conclusions about profitability.

Practical Advice

Value Betting Tips

Six habits that separate long-term winners from casual bettors.

  • Don't chase steam moves

    A steam move is when sharp bettors pile into a selection and the odds shorten rapidly across all books. By the time the move is visible, the edge is usually gone. Our algorithm flags value before the market corrects, not after.

  • Bet early when edges are biggest

    Bookmakers open lines early with wider margins. Edges narrow as kickoff approaches and more money flows in. For most football value picks, the best odds are available in the 12 to 24 hours before the match.

  • Use a staking plan

    Flat stakes of 1% to 2% of your bankroll per bet protect you during losing runs. The Kelly criterion sizes stakes based on your estimated edge, which can accelerate growth but requires disciplined edge estimation. Either method beats random staking.

  • Track every bet

    A spreadsheet with stake, odds, result, and calculated EV for each bet is your most valuable tool. Without records, you can't tell if your edges are real or if you've just run hot. Review your results after every 100 bets at a minimum.

  • Value betting is a marathon

    Losing runs of 10 or 20 bets are normal, even with a genuine edge. Don't increase stakes to recover losses and don't abandon a strategy based on a short run. Judge your approach over hundreds of bets, not dozens.

  • Don't ignore draw markets

    Draw outcomes in football are the most commonly mispriced market. Most bettors and many bookmakers systematically underestimate the frequency of draws, particularly in evenly matched or defensively solid fixtures. Draw value appears more often than bettors expect.
